I was drawn to this movie because of a tangent in a book club where two of my friends were discussed it intently. I essentially had the entire thing spoiled, but that made me only want to watch it more! This movie is the definition of though provoking. Even though it didn't leave an impact on me in the way some other films have, and even though the ending didn't tie the best bow on the thematics for me, I could talk about this movie for at least as long as my S tier movies. And I know that for a fact because I talked about it for at least two hours before even watching it, then had an hour conversation outside the theater, and still am dying to talk about it more as it has settled in my brain.

So, what is this movie about? It asks one main question: How much can a person be different than you thought before it breaks your existing relationship? This question is explored through a set of characters who all react quite differently to Emma divulging the worst thing she's ever done. Her fiance Charley is shocked and struggles to come to terms with it. His best friend Mike mostly tries to keep the peace as his wife Rachel fills with righteous fury.

As the movie progresses, nobody is perfect. You kind of hate all the characters at least a little bit, and they certainly make you cringe (more out of pain than embarrassment). And that is one source of discussion. But the thing the movie does even better is leave so many things open to interpretation in a way where it genuinely feels like either option could be true.

My one gripe with the movie is its ending. I personally feel like this movie has a clear central theme or question. And I like my endings to put a final point on such a theme or question. It doesn't have to answer it, mind you, but it should highlight it in a way that has some finality. In this case, the ending is up to interpretation for sure, but not in a way that I think does a good job of highlighting the central question. I've seen that moment read in three different ways (SPOILERS):

1. Emma is once again displaying her tendency to avoid. She wants to pretend that none of this happened and start again. She tried the same move twice before, and while it worked the first time (during their meet cute), it clearly failed the second time, and it's destined to fail again.
2. Emma is actually a psychopath. Charlie has been going back and forth asking himself if this is true -- even playing devils advocate with himself -- and yet he is unable to see the truth and will be manipulated again.
3. After a story of complete disaster, the writers bless us with a chance of redemption. It isn't guaranteed or even likely, but the possibility that two people can move past something like this is beautiful regardless.

Of the three options, I most agree with number one, but the scene isn't shot in a way that makes me feel like the director was seriously considering this perspective, and this ending doesn't really highlight the thematic question at all anyways. Number two adds to the idea that every single character can be seen multiple ways but again does not add to the thematic question. Number three relates the most to the theme, but is so absurdly off the mark (in my opinion) that it strains credulity and therefore doesn't really offer much more to a realistic discussion of the theme.

With that criticism out of the way, I must also give this film its due praise. The film choices in this movie are amazing. The way flashbacks are done is just perfect. It feels so intuitive as you go through the timeline of it despite the fact that it's somewhat convoluted. In this I credit the sound effects the most. They aren't your stereotypical sound cue (e.g. the reversed sound effects in LOST). Instead, they use drastic changes in the ambient noise of scenes to pull you out of the moment and force you to re-contextualize. If the flashback is on a noisy street, the cut to present day will be to a quiet scene and vice versa.

The second thing they do really well is a few shots where you essentially see characters hopes and/or fears playing out in their heads. Charlie has lots of fears about Emma after learning her secret and we see them play out on the screen just as they do in his head before snapping back to reality. But even more impressive are Emma's scenes where she imagines how she wants a moment to go just before it happens. Here the movie is cutting from an imagined version of a scene to the actual reality. Since the setting is the same, it's hard to communicate that what we just saw was an imagined reality. And yet the movie does this flawlessly. One of film's inabilities compared to other mediums like books is that seeing inside to a character's thoughts is almost impossible, but this movie accomplishes it anyways.

Overall this movie gave me so much to think about. Philosophically it asks you a lot of questions and provides very compelling hypotheticals to consider. From a film perspective it also just rocks so many elements of the process including writing, acting, directing, sound effects, and more. Ultimately, __The Drama__ has an amazing baseline and potential for complexity (which is what earns it an A), but it doesn't drive home its message/question with the full force that would warrant an S tier. Regardless, it is absolutely worth a watch.

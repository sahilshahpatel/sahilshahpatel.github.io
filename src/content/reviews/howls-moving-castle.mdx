I was recommended this book by my middle school librarian, and I remember loving it a lot. When the movie came out (or at least when I became aware of it), I was very excited, and I also remember loving that. I do also distinctly remember thinking the two were quite different. But in the interim 15 or so years I watched the movie enough times that the book faded from my memory, and while I recalled that they were different I couldn't remember how exactly. Well, I have now re-read the book, and while my memory of the movie remains a bit hazy, I definitely know some of the differences!

The first thing that struck me about the book was its sort of mundane attitude towards living in a fantastical world. I don't know what else to call that style, but I really loved how much Sophie just thinks her life is determined by birth order. As much magic and potential as there is in the world, Sophie has this innate assumption that none of it can ever be hers. The movie gives her a similar story, but it portrays it perhaps more realistically. I found the tongue-in-cheek nature of the book to be both funny and well tailored to the story it was trying to tell.

The next big thing I hadn't remembered flashes forward quite a bit: Howl is from Wales! I had completely forgotten that there was any mention of the real world in the book, and it took me by surprise for sure. I'm the end, I'm not sure exactly what purpose it serves in a thematic sense, and so I see why it was cut for the movie.

And finally I want to talk about the ending. The last two pages or so were just perfect. I can so picture in my head this scene of everyone wanting to talk to Howl and Sophie about the big things that just happened and what's next, meanwhile the two of them only have ears for each other. And they don't spend that time in a cinematic embrace or kiss, but instead they are just lightly flirting, the same way they have been throughout the book even if Sophie never realized. For a book that targets younger readers I think it's so perfect, because it encapsulates the non-physical parts of love so well, and is also a big character moment for Sophie as she chooses to focus on herself for just a moment.

And then the book ends, happy to freeze that final moment in perpetuity. I was curious how the book would end because I think Ghibli endings are so unique, and having read it again now I see that connection. The book's ending really does feel like a Ghibli ending in a way I haven't seen elsewhere, and it was perfect for the story.

Would this be an S if it weren't for nostalgia? Probably not, but only because it's target audience is clearly younger than I am now. But thinking back to my younger self I can see exactly why I loved this book. It's funny, simple, and whimsical. And I think I fancy myself as a bit of a Sophie if I'm honest. So at the time and for its target audience I think this book is absolutely an S.

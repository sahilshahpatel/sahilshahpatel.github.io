I was quite shocked by how much other people in the theater liked this movie when I watched it. I watched with three friends, one who had seen the movie before and none who had read the book. I myself had read the book years back (when it came out, I believe), and maybe that was why I didn't like the movie as much? But honestly I don't think so.

My main complaint is that the movie felt like a set of story beats and not a proper story. I've had this complaint more often recently where I feel like movies sometimes short cut the real emotional work of making me care about a character and instead just show me the outline of a story that would be cool if I did fully understand the characters, but isn't because I don't. In that way, having read the book actually gave me a leg up, which is why I was surprised my friends liked it so much.

The flashback scenes as Dr. Grace remembers the circumstances which brought him to space are key to the reader empathizing with him and understanding him in the book. One of my key concerns for a movie adaptation was how they were going to handle those. While the scenes themselves were pretty good in the movie, I don't think it did a good job at all of tying them back to the "present". The sequencing of it just didn't drive that point home well at all because Grace basically never has a moment of reflection after a memory comes back to him, it kind of just cuts to the next thing. And while they do make it clear he has amnesia, they also don't really focus on that all that much, which is odd given how crucial it is to the character arc that Grace goes through. One piece of praise I'll give this part is the cinematography of the memories being off-kilter to start with and then orienting before really starting. I think that was a brilliant idea and if it had just been paired with other more obvious clues that this was a sudden _new_ memory and also with some reflection about the memory after it surfaced, it would have been so awesome.

As usual the humor was hit or miss for me. I do love how Andy Weir seems to like to tie his "sciencing the shit out of this" characters with a sense of humor. He seems to believe that in order to survive situations like this one and like The Martian, one needs knowledge but one also needs the right attitude, and humor is an excellent coping mechanism. That being said, this movie really needed some of the more grounded and emotional moments I thought. Even The Martian movie had more moments of gravitas, and I think it needed more still.

Overall, my complaint isn't really about anything in the movie itself, it's more about what's missing. As someone who has read the book, there were definitely moments I missed for how cool they are (e.g. the physical mechanism behind the astrophage and the description of how Rocky's language works), but those omissions are not what I'm talking about. A movie adaptation should remove the details in areas like that in order to keep a tight story with depth in the right areas but in a smaller package. Unfortunately this movie still did not accomplish that in my view. Despite all that it removed, what remained was a skeleton of a story without the muscle to support it. The result of that is a movie that has emotional beats, but does not convey them with the depth they deserve. So while it seems popular perception would put this movie as an A+, I think it's a B at best for me.

Whiplash is obviously a famous movie, at least in music circles. And for the most part I feel that people understand the point it's trying to make fairly well. Interestingly though, I have met a few people who really don't, and so I want to go over the moments which I think confirm that the intent of the movie is for you to feel bad for Andrew and to think that he makes a mistake in going back to perform with Fletcher.

First, there is the obvious sign. During the movie, there is literally a lawsuit happening alleging abuse. I don't really see why this would be a part of the movie if we weren't to be on the lawsuit's side. There isn't any part of the film which guides you to think the lawsuit is a scam or manipulation.

Second is the girlfriend, Nicole. I could easily see someone asking why she is even in the movie. Certainly she exists to show us some of Andrew's transformation as a generic supporting character would. But think about what exactly she shows. Andrew's self worth is tied to his music career and nothing else, and we are shown that in almost every scene with Nicole. Andrew only asks her out after making the studio band. He breaks up with her when Connolly takes his spot in the band because he doesn't value her at all compared to his career. And he calls her again only once he's back in a show. If the story wanted us to believe that Nicole was truly holding him back, it would have shown some moment where he truly had to choose between her and his career. Instead Andrew only _thinks_ that he must choose, and we as the viewer see that it's to his detriment.

Third and most easily missed is his father. We don't see a lot of Andrew's father, but we generally see him in a good light. We know Andrew's mother died and his father has raised him alone since. When things are at their most intense with Fletcher, we know his dad does his research to find the lawyer and has seen the struggles his son is going through without him saying anything and even from a distance. The "worst" we see of Andrew's dad is at the dinner table, where he tempers his ego, and while it's not the most supportive choice, Andrew also kind of deserves it there. Generally I think these moments are supposed to make us believe that his dad truly wants the best for him. And that is what makes his small part in the finale all the more important. As Andrew goes back on stage in the end, his dad watches through the doors, and we see the pain on his face as he realizes that his son is truly lost. The fact that the movie chooses to highlight the dad in this scene and in that context is meaningful, and I believe it definitively points to a portrayal of Andrew's return to the stage as a failure, or at least a tragedy.

So that's why I think the movie's message is that singular focus on just one metric of success in life is damaging intrinsically and also opens you up to abuses which will be tolerated for fear that resistance will eliminate the one thing you have based your identity on. That message in and of itself is great, and the fact that it is presented with such complexity is another bonus. I felt the tension of this movie viscerally as I watched it, and that is impressive in its own right. For all of those reasons and more, this movie gets an A.

As a small aside, in my opinion the exact factual accuracy of a movie like this isn't too important. I did have a lot of fun pointing out moments where the music lingo didn't exactly make sense, but ultimately the movie captures a _feeling_ very accurately even if the exact facts of it are not realistic. And in my view that is sort of the point of stories, and so I would consider this movie a great success.

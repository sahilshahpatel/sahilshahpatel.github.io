I intended to write reviews for each book in this series as I read it, but it's a little late for that so here we are.

I was told before reading this trilogy that the first book "had no plot" but that the characters were so amazing that you didn't care. Personally, I don't think that's really true. The first book presents a clear villain and has a mystery plot as well which definitely drive interest. However it is true that the book is very character driven. Unlike many fantasy stories, you don't meet the characters just as they find out about a grand new adventure. Instead you meet them while they're doing their everyday thing. You learn about who they are and what they are like, and you're gripped by the idea of them as a person living in this world. After all of that is established, some interesting things start to happen which draw you into a larger plot.

The one criticism I have to give is a lack of well-written women in the book. Each woman in the book feels really tropey. I suppose the men might too, honestly, but the men also get much more agency in the story than the women, so it doesn't feel as bad. There isn't "negative representation" necessarily, but it also didn't feel like a shining example of women in fantasy.

The first book in this trilogy grips you with characters and the promise of an interesting if traditional plot. The second book explores new relationships between characters as they go on a joint adventure (and a new side of Glokta as he goes on his own adventure). The third book is where it gets really good though, especially at the end. (I'll keep it vague to avoid spoilers)

There is a twist in this book which I kind of saw coming, but I also didn't think the author would dare do. The series starts out with this medieval fantasy charm that lasts through much of book two with only a few questionable moments. But as we get into book three we start to see sides of familiar characters that we don't love so much, and this makes for such a good twist, especially for anyone feeling particularly disenfranchised or anti-establishment at the moment. What I love most about this is how well it fits into everything that came before, even though you never thought of it like that at the time. And that it's not entirely pessimistic about humans generally, it's only pessimistic about how the system continually suppresses our best parts and amplifies our worst.

My one gripe is that as a trilogy (whose successive books apparently have a time jump in them), I didn't love that the very very end felt so much like a setup for what's next. I think the twist of this third book was so good and hit home the theme so strongly that I would have loved for the focus to be on that until the very end. I feel that even in doing so paths for future stories in the world could easily have been left open.

Overall I would highly recommend this trilogy. It's not for everyone -- you have to like fantasy enough to enjoy the first two novels and like dark themes and politics enough to enjoy the third. But if it seems at all up your alley, it's absolutely astounding.

Quick shoutout to the audio books as well! The voice actor does an exquisite job. I listened to the first one on recommendation, read the second one because I wanted to pay more close attention, but went back to listening for the third since he was just so good.

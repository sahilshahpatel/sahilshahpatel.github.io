Sea of Tranquility was nothing like I expected. I had only read Station Eleven before and wasn't prepared for the references to Mandel's other books or the self-insert of Olive. All of that added up to a reading experience that injected a novelty that the plot itself needed quite a bit.

I loved Station Eleven in large part due to the writing style. I find Mandel's writing to be quite dream-like and ephemeral. I compare it to Denis Villeneuve's films in that both take focus off of specific events and redirect that attention to strong atmosphere. Sea of Tranquility absolutely has that same quality. The book starts with Edwin who's malaise really sets a kind of mundane and purposefully boring tone. Then, just as something big happens, we switch perspectives. Mirella's story is a lot more inherently interesting, but since its all about something that happened in the past, there is still this distance maintained from the plot that keeps it from feeling at all visceral. It is Olive's and Gaspery's chapters that feel the most vivid. That distance allows atmosphere to be noticed strongly, and it also sharpens Gaspery's story by contrast.

Olive's chapters were quite interesting for a few reasons. For one, they discuss the feeling of living through Covid-19 so eloquently. She captures the fear, the captivity, the normality of day-to-day life. It's also fascinating because it quickly becomes clear that Olive is a self-insert by the author. She is talking about her own experiences, not just by proxy but by quotation essentially. I have not read another work of auto-fiction, and so the process of realizing what it was and then absorbing it was quite interesting!

Finally Gaspery. I love and hate reading time travel stories. They present such interesting questions, and have so much potential. But it is also so hard to write them well, both because any attempt at explaining the mechanics is unlikely to go well and because it is almost impossible to tie a bow on the story that nearly wraps it all up. In the end, I don't think this book does an amazing job at using time travel for its thematic goals. But I do really love the philosophical realm that Gaspery ends up in, the titular Sea of Tranquility.

Ultimately, this book held my attention, and was satisfying to complete. Along the way there were a few thoughts that I think will stick with me, even if they didn't stick very strongly to the plot itself. Namely, that bureaucracy exists to preserve itself (which I'm sure I believe, and I'm not entirely sure I understand, but feels profound nonetheless). The book's short length suits it well so that it can get across it's points and spotlight it's characters for exactly as long as they have something to offer. I don't think it's as cohesive a story as Station Eleven, and I would never recommend it first, but I might recommend it second, and it certainly yielded a great discussion in book club. So for that, I give it an A-.

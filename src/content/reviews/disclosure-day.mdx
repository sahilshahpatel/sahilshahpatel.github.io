# The Premise
I read an article about Disclosure Day before watching it. The article talked about the film's desire to explore the mutual exclusivity of religion and aliens. The article and I mostly agreed -- we couldn't really see why the existence of aliens would be an existential threat to religion as a concept. After reading the article I was slightly curious how the film would make its point, but I also didn't have much faith it would be good and wasn't intent on seeing it. In the end I did watch it on a whim with a friend, and it was bad in even more ways than just that. But I do think there is latent potential in the idea, and I want to discuss it.

In my view, Disclosure Day is attempting to discuss how humans would react to proof of the existence of aliens philosophically. There are three characters that embody positions humanity might take:
- Noah Scanlon, the head of WARDEX, believes that we could not handle this because it would add to the chaos we already have and drive division
- Jane Blakenship, a former nun, believes that we could not handle this because it would mean we aren't special and all of our moral systems (read: Christianity -- more on that later) would collapse
- Hugo Wakefield, a former top WARDEX guy, believes that we could handle this and that humans are more empathetic and cooperative than we allow ourselves to imagine

This is a fairly interesting trichotomy on its face: the practical pessimist, the religious doomsayer, and the idealistic dreamer. Unfortunately, our two main characters aren't even featured in the list above, and that's where the problems really begin.

# Two Competing Storylines
The plot of this movie is all about the existence of aliens and this philosophical debate. But the emotional journey of the movie is about a completely different topic: empathy. The entire discussion above is pretty agnostic to the question "what kind of aliens are these?" In fact, a story like that might be best told without us meeting the aliens face-to-face but rather just acquiring proof. But Spielberg's aliens are these hyper empathetic creatures which are here to teach us something.

There is a version of this movie which ties the empathy ideas into the question of how we would react and ties them all together nicely. That version of the movie is called _Arrival_, it came out in 2016, and it's currently my favorite movie of all time. Ok, perhaps that's unfair. They aren't the same movie at all really, but _Arrival_ does a much better job of tying the global story of alien arrival with the personal story of its two main characters.

_Arrival_ also does a better job of making its two main characters foils of each other. Disclosure Day does create a dichotomy between Margaret and Daniel, but it's not a very useful one. In fact, it just propagates both the trope that math-y people aren't empathetic and that women have to be the empathetic ones. Instead, Margaret and Daniel should have had personal stories which somehow reflected the global story being told and offered a personal perspective on it. Perhaps one could have been jaded and on a path towards Noah's beliefs. Perhaps the other could have been entirely replaced by Jane so that we could have our obvious person-to-global connection be used at all effectively.

# A Tangent on Religion
On the topic of Jane, I did really like her introduction and how religion was tied into this movie initially. I thought it felt pretty natural and also brought up so many questions and so much potential for philosophical conflict. But the way it actually comes to a head is so fake. She watches these films of aliens being tortured and her immediate reaction is "people can't know this. If they do, religion will die!" Like, what? 1) It feels like there should be a stronger initial reaction to aliens existing and 2) why would religions die? Seriously, I really needed this movie to explain to me why one would even think that the existence of aliens would cause religion to die. That does not strike me as obvious -- in fact it strikes me as incredibly unlikely and stupid. The movie took a lot of pride (it seemed) in the gotcha of man being God's prime creation "on Earth" which allows for Christianity to continue. But how does that quote really address the base issue of religion in a world of alien intelligence? Sure, for the most literal of followers that might be enough, but for most Christians this wouldn't really address the philosophical and theological questions I don't think.

And now let's get back to that idea of Christianity as our moral system. I do believe that's true for many people, but other moral systems obviously exist, whether that's other religions or semi- or non-religious moral systems. The movie really only addresses Christianity, and even then only really by its literal text. It pays lip service to the idea of tying Jane's story to the global one (she didn't lose faith in God, she lost faith in people), but it doesn't actually do the leg work of integrating that into the story or even expanding on what that means.

# Other Complaints
My main complaint about this movie is this large scale thematic failure. But there are other weird things about it too. The movie features the most McGuffin-y McGuffin I've seen in a while with this magic stick that does whatever is required of it for no explained reason. Not only that, but it's supposedly dangerous (though we never really see that), and it also gets used up like fuel (but only when the plot demands it so that Jane can be somewhat relevant in the last two minutes). It also features a Chekhov's Gun which never gets fired in the WARDEX second-in-command. He is shown resisting Margaret and going against Noah's orders part way through the movie. So at the end when Noah again seems incapacitated (unconvincingly mind you), I thought for sure the second in command would do something. But instead he just walks away because I guess we're running out of time. The second-in-command also gets dusted by the McGuffin at some point but just reappears a few moments and that is never brought back up again, almost as if the editors cut out some section of the movie and forgot to remove the relevant breadcrumbs.

Besides those basic storytelling things, the movie also just feels old. Why are they running around with piles of USB sticks and going to the news for this? Where is social media? This is just not how any of this would realistically play out today. Also the news anchor talks about how they would only show verified news, but then just puts up all this footage live which they absolutely did not verify at all. So the basic idea of the plot just felt a bit half-baked.

# A Few Positives
My friend also complained about the very end. He said the movie promised to talk about how humanity reacts to proof of aliens, but then it ends before you can see that reaction in any way. This is one thing I'll give Spielberg, actually. To me, the idea of a movie which asks a hypothetical question, debates it through the story of the characters throughout the movie, and ends just before the answer is seen is quite intriguing. If the movie is done well, you'll be convinced of what will happen afterwards without needing to see it. But this movie does not convince you of anything at all, and it often doesn't even feel like it's trying.

That was a half-way positive I guess. The only real positive I can give this movie is that the acting performances were great. The characters and plot definitely lended themselves to interesting acting scenarios, and the actors took that and ran with it. Emily Blunt and Eve Hewson in particular had great performances.

# Wrapping it Up
There's a lot of potential here for sure, but almost all of it is squandered. I didn't expect this movie to be good after reading that article, but I was honestly a bit shocked by how many basic storytelling things were dropped. I cannot really recommend this movie to anyone except as a lesson in what not to do. But it is also clearly created by someone who knows the craft well. In the end it felt like a jigsaw puzzle where each piece was masterfully crafted and beautiful, but the artist had focused so much on the individual pieces that they forgot the end goal was to have them fit together. And so we are left with a box of puzzle pieces which physically cannot come together to a cohesive whole. C tier might honestly be generous for this movie, but given its acting and cinematography chops, I think I'll leave it at least there.

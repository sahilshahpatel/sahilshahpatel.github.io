I was very close to giving this book an S-. It's so unique in its storytelling, it's got many themes and messages which speak to me strongly, and it's a classic that I actually enjoyed (bonus points for that). I know that this is a book many people were read as kids, and as I read it I was imagining myself reading it to my future kids. There were so many reasons why I would love to do so, but ultimately the book is held back by its age. Despite being feminist for its time, it still portrays a lot of ideas that in my view are sexist. While an older reader can easily understand these to be remnants of an older culture, I fear young readers may internalize those ideas. This single aspect demotes the book from S- to A+.

That's still a quite positive rating though, so let's focus on what I liked. Firstly is the writing style. I can't find the right description, but it's like a parable or a fairy tale. Something like Aesop's fables. The only other book I've read with a similar style is Watership Down. Somehow this style softens what would otherwise be an immensely preachy book into something very enjoyable. Telling a story over years as characters mature is also quite difficult, and I think the writing style enables that very strongly.

I also liked a lot of the messaging of the story. While it was perhaps a tad religious for me, I do wholeheartedly believe that wealth should not be the goal, that kindness and love are the best gifts we can give, and that relationships take hard work. These are all explored through the stories of the little women. There are a few things which could be sexist, but I didn't view as such. Particularly, I really liked that each girl had a castle -- a dream or ambition for the future -- and that none really achieved exactly what they set out for. On the one hand, it definitely felt like a hampering of female ambition. But on the other hand I often think our world is filled with way too much ambition. The overall message about how to live your life and what brings true happiness was one I could easily get behind.

I do want to document my complaints at least in part. There were several moments which felt sexist to me, but the one that stands out is Mrs. March's advice to Meg concerning her marital problems. In my view the problem was one of communication, but Mrs. March prescribes that Meg put in all the effort to put things right. This is so unfair, so sexist, and also probably not even a good solution. It works out in this story, but I don't know that it would in real life.

With all that said, I would absolutely recommend Little Women. Not to read to children, unfortunately, but as an adult it is a truly heartwarming tale which could ease an overwhelmed soul.
